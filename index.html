Understanding Step 5: The Ionosphere

The ionosphere is a region of the upper atmosphere (about 50 to 1000 km) heavily ionized by solar radiation. This gas behaves as a giant plasma shell, refracting (bending) HF radio waves back to Earth.

D Layer 50 – 90 km

The Sponge: Dominated by dense air particles. It does not refract signals; instead, it absorbs low-frequency HF waves. It completely disappears at night as recombination takes place rapidly.

E Layer 90 – 140 km

Medium Refractor: Highly active during the day. Bends medium-frequency waves. At night, it weakens drastically but occasionally supports "Sporadic E" propagation.

F1 Layer 140 – 210 km

Daytime Split: Forms only during daylight. Works in conjunction with the F2 layer. Merges back with F2 as the sun sets.

F2 Layer > 210 km

Long-Distance Engine: The highest and most ionized layer. Responsible for the longest skip communication (DX). Stays active 24/7 (merging into a single F layer at night).

Key Frequency Rules:

  • Critical Frequency ($f_c$): The absolute highest frequency that returns if sent straight up (90°). Higher than this penetrates into space.
  • MUF (Maximum Usable Frequency): The maximum frequency that returns at a given angle. If you transmit above MUF, your wave escapes.
  • LUF (Lowest Usable Frequency): The minimum frequency needed to pierce the absorbing D-layer without being completely lost.
